This book explores the intersection of cybernetics and digital autonomy in the modern era. As closed ecosystems and proprietary software increasingly dominate the landscape, understanding the theoretical underpinnings of open systems becomes critical for preserving user agency.

We analyze historical paradigms of computing, drawing parallels between early distributed networks and modern decentralized protocols. The core thesis posits that software architecture is inherently political, and technical decisions regarding data sovereignty, encryption, and interoperability directly dictate the extent of a user's digital autonomy.
